The IRS Has a Deadline to Collect

Most taxpayers don't know this: the IRS has exactly 10 years from the date of tax assessment to collect a tax debt. This deadline is called the Collection Statute Expiration Date (CSED). Once it passes, the debt is legally extinguished — the IRS cannot collect, levy, garnish, or sue to recover it.

✅ What Happens at the CSED
When the 10-year statute expires, the IRS must release all federal tax liens, stop all collection activity, and the debt is permanently eliminated — no bankruptcy required, no payment necessary.

When Does the 10-Year Clock Start?

The CSED starts on the date the IRS officially assesses the tax — not the date you filed or the due date. For most returns, assessment happens within weeks of filing. For audits, assessment happens when the audit results are finalized.

What PAUSES the CSED (Tolling Events)?

The 10-year clock is not always continuous. Certain events pause ("toll") the statute and extend your total collection window:

Tolling EventDuration Tolled
Offer in Compromise pendingDuration of review + 30 days
Installment Agreement pendingDuration + 30 days
Bankruptcy filingDuration + 6 months
CDP Hearing requestDuration of hearing
Military service in combat zoneDuration + 180 days
Taxpayer Advocate caseDuration of case
Living outside the US (6+ months)Duration of absence
⚠️ The OIC Trap
Filing an Offer in Compromise pauses your CSED for the entire review period plus 30 days. If your OIC takes 18 months to review and reject, you just added 18 months to your collection window. Factor this into your strategy.

How to Find Your CSED

You can find your CSED by:

  • Ordering a Tax Account Transcript from IRS.gov (shows assessment date)
  • Calling the IRS at 1-800-829-1040
  • Having a tax professional pull your IRS transcript and calculate the exact CSED including all tolling events

Using the CSED as a Strategy

If your CSED is 2–3 years away and you have limited assets, getting into Currently Not Collectible status or a minimal payment plan allows the statute to continue running. When it expires, the debt vanishes. A tax professional can calculate whether waiting out the statute is better than settling or paying.

💡 Real Strategy Example
Taxpayer owes $180,000 from 2016. CSED is 2026. Gets into CNC status in 2024. IRS cannot collect. In 2026, the debt expires completely. Total paid: $0. This is legal and it happens.
