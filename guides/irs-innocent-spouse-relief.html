What Is Innocent Spouse Relief?

When married couples file joint tax returns, both spouses are jointly and severally liable for any taxes owed — even if only one spouse earned the income or made the error. Innocent Spouse Relief is a provision that allows you to be relieved of responsibility for taxes, penalties, and interest resulting from your spouse's errors or omissions that you didn't know about.

✅ Who This Helps
Divorced or separated taxpayers, spouses who didn't review the return before signing, spouses who were unaware of unreported income, and victims of financial abuse by a spouse.

Three Types of Innocent Spouse Relief

1. Classic Innocent Spouse Relief

If there is a tax understatement due to erroneous items of your spouse, and you did not know (or had no reason to know) about it when you signed the return, you may qualify. You must apply within 2 years of IRS collection activity.

2. Separation of Liability Relief

The IRS splits the tax liability between you and your spouse based on each person's contribution to the understated tax. You only pay your portion. You must be divorced, legally separated, widowed, or have not lived with your spouse for 12 months.

3. Equitable Relief

If you don't qualify for the first two, you may still get relief if it would be unfair to hold you responsible based on your overall circumstances. This covers both underpayments and understatements.

How to Apply: IRS Form 8857

File Form 8857 (Request for Innocent Spouse Relief) with the IRS. The IRS will notify your spouse or ex-spouse and give them a chance to respond. The process typically takes 6+ months. A tax professional can prepare and submit this on your behalf.

⚠️ Don't Miss the Deadline
For Classic and Separation of Liability relief, you have only 2 years from the date the IRS first attempted to collect. For Equitable Relief, the deadline is extended. Missing these windows means you lose the right to relief.

Factors the IRS Considers

  • Whether you knew or should have known about the understatement
  • Whether you benefited from the underreported income
  • Whether you've since divorced or separated
  • Your current financial situation
  • Whether you were a victim of abuse or financial control
