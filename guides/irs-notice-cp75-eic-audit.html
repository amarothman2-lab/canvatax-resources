What Is IRS Notice CP75?

IRS Notice CP75 means the IRS has selected your Earned Income Credit (EIC) claim for examination. They need you to prove that you are eligible for the credit — primarily by verifying that the qualifying children listed on your return actually meet the IRS's requirements for age, residency, and relationship.

⚠️ Your Refund Is On Hold
When you receive CP75, any refund tied to the EIC is frozen until the examination is resolved. The IRS will not release your refund until you respond with sufficient documentation.

The Three Tests for a Qualifying Child

1. Relationship Test

The child must be your son, daughter, stepchild, foster child, sibling, or descendant of any of these. Include adopted children.

2. Age Test

Under 19 at end of year, OR under 24 if a full-time student, OR any age if permanently and totally disabled.

3. Residency Test

The child must have lived with you in the US for more than half the tax year. This is the most commonly questioned requirement.

Documents That Prove EIC Eligibility

What to ProveAcceptable Documents
Child's identityBirth certificate, Social Security card, passport
Residency (child lived with you)School records with your address, medical records, lease/mortgage with child listed, childcare provider records
RelationshipBirth certificate, adoption papers, foster care records
Your qualifying incomeW-2s, 1099s, self-employment records
💡 The Strongest Evidence for Residency
School records are the gold standard — they show where the child was enrolled, which implies where they lived. Combine with medical records and a lease agreement listing the child at your address for the strongest possible case.

How to Respond to CP75

  1. Gather all documents listed above
  2. Complete any forms included with the CP75 (usually Form 886-H-EIC)
  3. Make copies (keep originals)
  4. Send by certified mail to the address on the notice
  5. Respond within the deadline — typically 30 days from the notice date

If You Can't Provide Sufficient Documentation

If documentation is incomplete, a tax professional may be able to supplement with alternative evidence — sworn statements, school registration records, or government benefit records. The IRS will consider all relevant evidence.
