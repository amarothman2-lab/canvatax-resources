What Is an Offer in Compromise?

An Offer in Compromise (OIC) is an agreement between you and the IRS that allows you to settle your tax debt for less than the full amount owed. If accepted, the remaining balance is forgiven. It is one of the most powerful IRS debt relief tools available — and one of the most misunderstood.

✅ Real Example
A taxpayer owes $85,000 in IRS back taxes. After submitting an OIC based on their income and assets, the IRS accepts $11,200 as full settlement. The remaining $73,800 is wiped clean.

Three Grounds for an Offer in Compromise

1. Doubt as to Collectibility (Most Common)

You cannot pay the full amount owed within the remaining collection period. The IRS calculates your Reasonable Collection Potential (RCP) — the most they could ever collect from you. If your RCP is less than what you owe, you may qualify.

2. Doubt as to Liability

You dispute whether you actually owe the tax debt. This is rare and requires evidence the assessment was incorrect.

3. Effective Tax Administration

You could technically pay but doing so would create an economic hardship or be unfair based on exceptional circumstances.

How the IRS Calculates Your Offer Amount

Your minimum offer is based on your Reasonable Collection Potential (RCP):

📊 OIC Formula
RCP = Net Asset Value + Future Income Potential

Net Asset Value = Your assets (bank accounts, equity, vehicles) minus exempt amounts
Future Income = Monthly disposable income × 12 (lump sum) or × 24 (periodic payment)
Payment TypeFormulaProcessing Time
Lump Sum (paid within 5 months)RCP × 12 + net assets~6–12 months
Periodic Payment (paid over 6–24 months)RCP × 24 + net assets~12–18 months

OIC Acceptance Rate — The Truth

The IRS accepts roughly 30–40% of all submitted OICs. However, professionally prepared offers have significantly higher acceptance rates. Many rejected OICs fail because of calculation errors, missing documentation, or unrealistic offer amounts.

What Happens While Your OIC Is Pending?

  • IRS collection activity is paused (no levies or garnishments)
  • The 10-year collection statute is tolled (paused)
  • You must continue filing tax returns and paying current taxes
  • You must make required payments with your offer submission
⚠️ OIC Pre-Qualifier
The IRS offers a free OIC Pre-Qualifier tool at IRS.gov. However, it is basic and often underestimates your chances. A licensed tax professional can identify deductions the tool misses and build a far stronger case.

What Disqualifies You from an OIC?

  • You are currently in bankruptcy
  • You have unfiled tax returns
  • You have not made required estimated tax payments
  • You are a business owner with unfiled payroll tax returns
