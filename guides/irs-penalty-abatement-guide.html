What Is IRS Penalty Abatement?

IRS penalty abatement is the removal or reduction of penalties the IRS has added to your tax debt. Penalties can add 25–40% to your original balance. Abatement can save thousands of dollars and is often overlooked by taxpayers who don't know they can ask for it.

✅ Common Penalties That Can Be Removed
Failure-to-file penalty (5% per month, up to 25%), Failure-to-pay penalty (0.5% per month, up to 25%), Accuracy-related penalty (20% of underpaid tax), Failure-to-deposit penalty (for businesses), Return preparer penalties.

Three Types of IRS Penalty Abatement

1. First-Time Penalty Abatement (FTA)

This is the easiest and most common form of abatement. If you have a clean compliance history (no penalties in the past 3 years), the IRS will typically waive your first penalty — no documentation required. You just need to ask.

💡 FTA Is Underused
The IRS grants First-Time Abatement readily, yet most taxpayers never ask. Simply calling the IRS and requesting FTA — or having a tax professional do it — often results in immediate penalty removal.

2. Reasonable Cause Abatement

If you had a legitimate reason for failing to file or pay on time, the IRS may waive penalties. Qualifying reasons include:

  • Serious illness or injury (you or immediate family)
  • Natural disaster or fire destroying records
  • Death of immediate family member
  • Reliance on incorrect IRS advice
  • Unavoidable absence
  • Erroneous advice from a tax professional

3. Statutory Exception

If a penalty was assessed due to a change in tax law, erroneous IRS written advice, or a presidentially declared disaster, the IRS must remove it.

How to Request Penalty Abatement

You can request abatement by:

  1. Calling the IRS directly (takes 30–60 minutes on hold)
  2. Sending a written request by mail (takes 2–3 months for response)
  3. Filing Form 843 (Claim for Refund and Request for Abatement)
  4. Having a licensed tax professional call and negotiate on your behalf (fastest)
⚠️ Timing Matters
You generally have 2 years from the date of payment to request abatement by refund, and 3 years from the return due date. Don't wait — penalties accrue interest too.
