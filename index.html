1. Offer in Compromise

The Offer in Compromise (OIC) is the most powerful IRS debt relief program. It allows taxpayers to settle their entire federal tax debt — including back taxes, penalties, and interest — for less than the full amount owed.

The IRS accepts an OIC when they believe the offer represents the maximum they can realistically collect considering the taxpayer's income, expenses, and asset equity. In 2023, the IRS accepted over 13,000 offers with an average acceptance of approximately 20 cents on the dollar.

Who Qualifies?

  • Total assets and future income are less than the full tax liability
  • Current on all required tax filings
  • No open bankruptcy proceedings
  • Have made all required estimated tax payments

2. IRS Installment Agreement

An installment agreement allows taxpayers to pay their IRS debt over time — up to 72 months. Once approved, the IRS suspends wage garnishments and bank levies.

For debts under $50,000, taxpayers can apply online at IRS.gov without a financial review. For larger balances, a full financial disclosure is required. Monthly payments are based on disposable income after allowable living expenses.

3. Currently Not Collectible Status

If a taxpayer has no disposable income after necessary living expenses, the IRS will place their account in Currently Not Collectible (CNC) status. All collection activity — wage garnishments, bank levies, lien enforcement — is suspended for as long as CNC status remains active.

CNC does not eliminate debt, but the 10-year collection statute continues to run. Combined with penalty abatement or an eventual OIC, CNC status can be part of a complete resolution strategy.

4. IRS Penalty Abatement

IRS penalties — failure to file, failure to pay, accuracy penalties — can add 25–50% to a taxpayer's balance. The IRS has two main abatement programs:

  • First-Time Penalty Abatement: Available to taxpayers with no prior penalties in the last 3 years. Eliminates failure-to-file and failure-to-pay penalties entirely.
  • Reasonable Cause Abatement: Available when a taxpayer can demonstrate they had a legitimate reason for non-compliance — medical emergency, natural disaster, reliance on incorrect professional advice.

5. Stopping IRS Wage Garnishment

The IRS can garnish up to 70% of a taxpayer's disposable income without a court order. Garnishment continues every pay period until the debt is resolved or a formal resolution is established.

Methods to stop garnishment include establishing an installment agreement, filing an OIC (which suspends collection), proving financial hardship (CNC), or requesting a Collection Due Process hearing.

6. IRS Bank Levy Release

An IRS bank levy seizes funds directly from a taxpayer's bank account. The bank holds funds for 21 days before sending them to the IRS — this 21-day window is critical for resolution.

A levy can be released by establishing a payment plan, filing an OIC, or proving financial hardship. After the 21-day window, levy proceeds can be returned if the levy was wrongful or caused extreme hardship.

7. Federal Tax Lien Removal

A federal tax lien attaches to all property a taxpayer owns and is filed as public record. It damages credit and prevents property sales. Lien removal options include: full payment, discharge of specific property, subordination, withdrawal (available after entering Direct Debit Installment Agreement), or acceptance of an OIC.
