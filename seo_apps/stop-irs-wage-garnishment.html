Everything You Need to Know About IRS Wage Garnishment

What Is IRS Wage Garnishment?

IRS wage garnishment (officially called a "continuous wage levy") is when the IRS legally orders your employer to withhold a portion of your paycheck and send it directly to the IRS. Unlike a creditor garnishment, the IRS does not need a court order — they can garnish your wages with just an administrative notice.

How Much Can the IRS Take?

The IRS uses Publication 1494 to calculate your exempt amount — based on filing status and dependents. Everything above that exempt amount can be taken. For most people, this means the IRS takes 50–70% of every paycheck until the debt is paid or a resolution is reached.

How to Stop IRS Wage Garnishment

  • Installment Agreement: Most common solution. Set up a monthly payment plan and the IRS releases the garnishment same day the agreement is approved.
  • Offer in Compromise: If you qualify, the IRS suspends all collection including garnishment while your OIC is pending.
  • Currently Not Collectible (CNC): If you have no disposable income, the IRS is legally required to release the garnishment and pause all collection.
  • Collection Due Process Hearing: Requesting a CDP hearing within 30 days of the levy notice freezes all collection action while the appeal is pending.
  • Financial Hardship: If the garnishment prevents you from paying basic living expenses, the IRS must release it upon proper documentation.

What Happens If You Ignore IRS Garnishment?

The garnishment continues indefinitely — every pay period — until your full tax debt is paid or a resolution is established. Many people see 60–70% of their income seized for months or years. Beyond the financial damage, prolonged garnishment can lead to housing instability, inability to pay other debts, and cascading financial crisis.
