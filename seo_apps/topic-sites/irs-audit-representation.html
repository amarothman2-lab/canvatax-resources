What Is an IRS Audit?

An IRS audit is a review of your tax return to verify that reported income, deductions, and credits are accurate and comply with tax law. The IRS conducts roughly 700,000 audits per year — targeting returns that statistically deviate from norms for your income level and profession.

Types of IRS Audits

  • Correspondence Audit: Most common. IRS sends a letter requesting documentation for a specific item. Handled entirely by mail. Usually the least serious.
  • Office Audit: You (or your representative) meet with an IRS examiner at a local IRS office. Covers several return items. More thorough than a correspondence audit.
  • Field Audit: An IRS Revenue Agent comes to your home or business. Most comprehensive and most serious. Typically triggered by large balances, business income, or complex returns.

Most Common Audit Triggers

  • High deductions relative to income (especially home office, meals, travel)
  • Large Schedule C losses (hobby loss rules)
  • 100% business use of vehicle
  • Income mismatch (1099s reported don't match your return)
  • Large cash business with round revenue numbers
  • Foreign accounts or income
  • Prior audit history

Why Representation Matters

You have the right to have a licensed professional (Enrolled Agent, CPA, or tax attorney) represent you before the IRS — and they do not have to bring you to the meeting. Your representative handles all IRS questions, controls what information is provided, and negotiates any adjustments. Going in alone means you may volunteer information that expands the audit's scope.
