What Is a Collection Due Process Hearing?

A Collection Due Process (CDP) hearing is your legal right to contest IRS collection action before an independent IRS Office of Appeals — separate from the collection division that's trying to take your money. It's one of the most powerful tools in tax law, and most taxpayers don't know it exists until they're about to lose their paycheck or bank account.

When You Can Request a CDP Hearing

You have CDP rights when you receive specific IRS notices:

  • Notice of Federal Tax Lien Filing (Letter 3172): File Form 12153 within 30 days to contest the lien
  • Final Notice of Intent to Levy — LT11 or Letter 1058: File Form 12153 within 30 days to stop the levy

What Requesting a CDP Hearing Does Immediately

  • Suspends all levy action (wages, bank accounts, Social Security) while the hearing is pending
  • Stops new lien filings related to the hearing issue
  • Gives you the right to propose alternative collection resolutions
  • Preserves your right to appeal to Tax Court if the hearing is unfavorable

What You Can Raise at a CDP Hearing

  • Propose a payment plan, OIC, or CNC as an alternative to levy
  • Contest the validity of the underlying tax debt (if you never had a prior opportunity)
  • Argue that the collection action is an "abuse of discretion" by the IRS
  • Request lien withdrawal or subordination
  • Raise innocent spouse issues

After the 30-Day Window

If you miss the 30-day CDP deadline, you lose your right to a CDP hearing. You can still request an "equivalent hearing" (within 1 year of the notice), but this does NOT suspend collection — it's a weaker option. This is why acting on LT11 notices immediately is critical.
