How Divorce Creates IRS Tax Debt Problems

Joint tax returns mean joint liability — for both spouses, regardless of who earned the income, who made the financial decisions, or what your divorce decree says. The IRS is not a party to your divorce agreement. If your ex-spouse understated income or failed to pay taxes on a jointly filed return, the IRS can — and will — come after you for the full amount.

Your Divorce Decree Doesn't Protect You

Many divorce decrees include language like "Spouse A agrees to be responsible for the 2019 tax liability." That's enforceable between you and your ex-spouse in divorce court — but it means nothing to the IRS. If your ex doesn't pay, the IRS comes to you regardless of what your decree says. Your recourse is back to divorce court — while the IRS is already garnishing your wages.

Relief Programs for Divorced Taxpayers

  • Innocent Spouse Relief: If your ex understated income or claimed false deductions and you didn't know and had no reason to know, you may be fully absolved of liability. Applies when it would be "inequitable" to hold you responsible.
  • Separation of Liability Relief: Available only to divorced, legally separated, or widowed taxpayers. The IRS allocates the joint tax debt — you're only liable for your portion of the understatement. Doesn't require you to prove you didn't know, just that you're no longer with the person.
  • Equitable Relief: Broader catch-all. Applies even to underpayments (not just understatements) and when it's simply unfair to hold you liable given the full circumstances of the marriage and divorce.

Going Forward — Filing Status After Divorce

Once legally divorced (or separated under a formal decree), you cannot file jointly. Your options are Single or Head of Household (if you have dependents). This separates future liability entirely — but doesn't help with past joint returns.
