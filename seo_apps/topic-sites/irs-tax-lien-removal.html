What Is an IRS Tax Lien?

A federal tax lien is the IRS's legal claim against all your property — home, vehicles, bank accounts, business assets, and future property — as security for unpaid tax debt. The lien attaches automatically once you owe taxes and fail to pay after a demand. When the IRS files a Notice of Federal Tax Lien in public records, it becomes visible to lenders, title companies, and employers.

How Tax Liens Affect You

  • Blocks home sales (lien must be satisfied at closing)
  • Prevents mortgage refinancing (lenders won't approve)
  • Appears in public records searches
  • Can affect professional licenses and security clearances
  • Damages business credit and vendor relationships

4 Ways to Remove or Reduce an IRS Tax Lien

  • Full Payment: Pay the debt in full. IRS releases the lien within 30 days.
  • Lien Withdrawal: Available when you owe under $25,000 AND set up a Direct Debit installment agreement. The IRS removes the public Notice of Federal Tax Lien filing — even though you haven't paid in full. This is the most underused option and the fastest way to clean up public records.
  • Lien Discharge: Removes the lien from a specific piece of property — typically used when you need to sell your home. The IRS agrees to release that property from the lien even though the debt isn't fully paid.
  • Lien Subordination: The IRS agrees to take a lower priority position than your mortgage lender — allowing you to refinance while the lien remains on the account.

OIC and Lien Release

When an Offer in Compromise is accepted and the final payment is made, the IRS releases all associated tax liens within 30 days. This is one of the most complete outcomes in tax resolution — debt eliminated, lien gone, fresh start.
