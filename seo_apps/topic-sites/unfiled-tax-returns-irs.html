The Danger of Not Filing Tax Returns

Not filing a tax return is often more damaging than not paying. The failure-to-file penalty is 5% per month on your unpaid balance — up to 25%. That means in just 5 months, the penalty alone adds 25% to whatever you owe. And unlike failure-to-pay penalties, failure-to-file penalties are harder to remove through abatement.

What the IRS Does When You Don't File

If you have income reported under your Social Security number (W-2s, 1099s) and don't file, the IRS will eventually file a Substitute for Return (SFR) on your behalf using the worst possible assumptions — single filing status, no deductions, no credits. The result is a dramatically inflated tax bill.

How to Fix Unfiled Returns

  1. Pull IRS transcripts first. Download your Wage and Income Transcripts (shows all income reported under your SSN) and Account Transcripts (shows what IRS has already assessed). Go to IRS.gov → Get Your Tax Record.
  2. Reconstruct your income and expenses. Use transcripts for income figures. Gather receipts, bank statements, or estimates for deductions.
  3. File the actual returns. Your real return supersedes any IRS Substitute for Return. Most people owe significantly less once legitimate deductions are applied.
  4. Set up resolution simultaneously. File and have your payment plan or OIC application ready on the same day. Don't file and wait for a bill.

How Many Years Do You Need to File?

The IRS generally requires the last 6 years of unfiled returns to be filed before considering any resolution. However, they sometimes accept fewer years depending on your situation. CanvaTax determines the minimum filing requirement for your case.
