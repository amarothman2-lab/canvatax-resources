What Is Currently Not Collectible Status?

Currently Not Collectible (CNC) — also called IRS hardship status — is a formal IRS designation that suspends all collection activity when you genuinely cannot afford to pay without creating a financial hardship. Once granted, the IRS freezes levies, garnishments, and new enforcement actions.

What CNC Does

  • Immediately stops all wage garnishments
  • Immediately stops bank levies
  • Prevents new collection actions from being issued
  • Requires zero monthly payments while active
  • Allows the 10-year collection statute to continue running (meaning balance can expire)

What CNC Does NOT Do

  • Interest and penalties continue to accrue on the balance
  • The IRS will file a tax lien if they haven't already
  • CNC is reviewed periodically — if your income rises significantly, you'll be removed
  • It does not eliminate the debt (unlike an OIC)

Who Qualifies?

The IRS compares your monthly income to your allowable monthly expenses (using national and local standard tables). If your expenses equal or exceed your income — leaving zero disposable income — you qualify. The lower your income relative to living costs, the stronger your CNC case.

How to Apply

Submit Form 433-A (individuals) or 433-F (simplified version) with full income and expense documentation. For extremely low income situations, a phone call with the IRS (with documentation ready) can sometimes result in immediate CNC approval.

CNC as a Long-Term Strategy

For older debts (5+ years from assessment), CNC combined with the 10-year collection statute can be a powerful strategy. If you enter CNC with 3 years left on the statute and remain in CNC, the balance may legally expire without any payment. CanvaTax evaluates collection statute dates on every case.
