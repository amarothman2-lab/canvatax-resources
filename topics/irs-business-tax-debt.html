Why Business Tax Debt Is Different

Business IRS debt — especially payroll tax debt — is the most dangerous type of tax obligation an owner can face. Unlike personal income tax debt, payroll tax (Form 941) debt triggers the Trust Fund Recovery Penalty, which makes you personally liable as an individual, regardless of your corporate structure.

The Trust Fund Recovery Penalty (TFRP)

When a business collects payroll taxes from employees but doesn't remit them to the IRS, those funds are called "trust fund" taxes — money held in trust for the government. The TFRP equals 100% of the unpaid employee share and can be assessed personally against any "responsible person" — typically the owner, CFO, bookkeeper, or anyone with check-signing authority.

What "Responsible Person" Means

  • You had authority to pay company bills and make financial decisions
  • You were aware the taxes were not being paid
  • You continued to pay other creditors (suppliers, rent, employees) instead of the IRS

Emergency Action for Payroll Tax Debt

  • Stop accruing new payroll tax debt immediately — get current on all deposits
  • Do not co-mingle personal and business funds (IRS applies payments to oldest quarters first)
  • Do not meet with the IRS alone — get representation before any interview
  • Contest TFRP assessment within 60 days if you believe you were not a responsible person

Resolution Options for Business Tax Debt

  • Business installment agreement (Form 9465 for businesses)
  • Offer in Compromise for business (Form 433-B OIC)
  • Partial Pay Installment Agreement
  • TFRP contest — can eliminate personal liability entirely if successful
