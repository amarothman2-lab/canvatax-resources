What Is IRS Wage Garnishment?

IRS wage garnishment — technically called a "continuous wage levy" — is when the IRS instructs your employer to withhold a portion of every paycheck and send it directly to the IRS until your debt is paid. Unlike a bank levy (one-time), wage garnishment continues with every paycheck until it's formally released.

How Much Can the IRS Take?

The IRS uses Publication 1494 to calculate the exempt amount — the minimum you're allowed to keep. Everything above that exempt amount goes to the IRS. For most working adults, this means the IRS takes 50–70% of each paycheck. The calculation is based on filing status and number of dependents.

How to Stop IRS Wage Garnishment

  • Installment Agreement: The most common solution. Once the IRS approves a payment plan, they must release the garnishment. An experienced firm can get approval within 24–48 hours.
  • Offer in Compromise: Filing an OIC immediately suspends all levy activity including wage garnishment for the duration of the review period (typically 6–18 months).
  • Currently Not Collectible Status: If you cannot afford basic living expenses, the IRS is required to suspend collection including garnishment.
  • Collection Due Process Hearing: If you have unused CDP rights, requesting a hearing immediately suspends the garnishment.
  • Full Payment: Paying the balance in full releases the garnishment instantly.

What Your Employer Knows

The IRS sends a Notice of Levy directly to your employer's payroll department. Your employer is legally required to comply — they have no discretion. The notice includes your name and the amount to withhold. There is no way to prevent your employer from knowing once a levy is served.
